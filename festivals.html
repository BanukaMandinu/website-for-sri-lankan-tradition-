Events and Festivals in Sri Lanka

January

Duruthu Perahara


Duruthu Perahera is an annual religious and cultural festival held in Sri Lanka to commemorate the first visit of Gautama Buddha to the country. The festival is usually held in January during the full moon of the month of Duruthu (also known as Duruthu Poya) and is considered one of the most important events in the Sri Lankan Buddhist calendar. The festival features a grand procession known as the Duruthu Perahera, which takes place in the city of Kelaniya, near the capital city of Colombo. The procession includes beautifully decorated elephants, traditional dancers, drummers, and other performers dressed in colorful costumes. The highlight of the Duruthu Perahera is the sacred relic of the Buddha's tooth, which is carried on the back of a magnificent elephant, accompanied by dancers and musicians. The procession proceeds through the streets, and thousands of people come out to watch and participate in the festivities. The Duruthu Perahera is a celebration of Sri Lanka's rich cultural heritage and a time for people to come together in joy and celebration. It is a time to pay homage to the Buddha and to reflect on the teachings of Buddhism, which emphasizes the importance of compassion, wisdom, and kindness towards all beings.

Thai Pongal


Thai Pongal is a harvest festival celebrated by Tamils in Sri Lanka and other parts of the world. The festival is usually celebrated in January, around the same time as the Duruthu Perahera, and is a time for giving thanks for the bountiful harvest and expressing gratitude to the sun god, Surya. In Sri Lanka, Thai Pongal is celebrated with great enthusiasm and involves a range of traditions and customs. One of the most important traditions is the preparation of Pongal, a sweet rice dish made with rice, jaggery, and milk. The dish is typically cooked outdoors in a clay pot over an open fire, and is a symbol of the abundance of the harvest. Another important tradition is the decoration of homes and temples with colourful rangolis (patterns made with coloured rice or powder) and the use of fresh flowers. People also wear new clothes, exchange gifts and visit friends and family during this festive time. Thai Pongal is also a time for prayers and rituals, with devotees offering prayers and making offerings to the sun god, thanking him for the abundant harvest and seeking his blessings for a prosperous future. Overall, Thai Pongal is an important festival for Tamils in Sri Lanka and is a celebration of gratitude, abundance, and unity.



February

Independence day


Sri Lanka Independence Day is celebrated on February 4th every year to commemorate the country's independence from British colonial rule in 1948. The day is a national holiday in Sri Lanka and is marked by a series of celebrations, parades, and ceremonies. The main Independence Day celebrations are held in the capital city of Colombo, where a grand military parade takes place, with members of the armed forces, police, and other uniformed services marching in formation. The parade is usually presided over by the President of Sri Lanka, who also delivers a speech to the nation. In addition to the military parade, there are also cultural performances, including traditional dances, music, and other forms of entertainment. Schools, government buildings, and private homes are decorated with the national flag and bunting in the colours of the national flag. The day is also marked by the singing of the national anthem and the hoisting of the national flag. In schools and government offices, students and employees gather to raise the flag and sing the anthem together as a symbol of national unity and pride. Sri Lanka Independence Day is an important national holiday that celebrates the country's freedom and independence, and is a time for people to come together in a spirit of unity and patriotism.

Maha Sivarathri


Maha Shivaratri is a Hindu festival celebrated annually in honour of Lord Shiva, one of the most important deities in Hinduism. The festival is observed on the 14th day of the dark fortnight in the Hindu month of Phalguna (usually around February or March) and is a night-long celebration. The word Maha Shivaratri literally means "Great Night of Shiva" and is believed to be the night when Lord Shiva performed the Tandava Nritya or the cosmic dance of creation, preservation, and destruction. Devotees fast and stay awake all night, offering prayers to Lord Shiva and seeking his blessings for good health, happiness, and prosperity. In Sri Lanka, Maha Shivaratri is celebrated by Hindu communities across the country. Temples dedicated to Lord Shiva are decorated with lights and flowers, and devotees gather to perform poojas and offer prayers throughout the night. Special offerings such as milk, honey, and fruits are made to Lord Shiva, and devotional songs and hymns are sung in his praise. The festival is also a time for devotees to reflect on the teachings of Lord Shiva, which emphasise the importance of self-realisation, detachment, and devotion. It is a time to seek spiritual upliftment and to connect with the divine through prayer, meditation, and introspection. Overall, Maha Shivaratri is a significant festival in Sri Lanka and is a time for devotees to celebrate and express their devotion to Lord Shiva. It is a time for spiritual renewal and for seeking the blessings of the divine.



April

Sinhala and Tamil New year


The Sinhala and Tamil New Year, also known as Aluth Avurudda, is a major cultural festival celebrated by the Sinhalese and Tamil communities in Sri Lanka. The festival falls in mid-April and is a time for celebrating the new year, as well as the spring harvest season. The festival is celebrated over several days and involves a range of customs and traditions. The first day of the festival is marked by the transition from the old year to the new year, which is determined by astrological calculations. The exact time of the transition is marked by the lighting of hearths and the preparation of traditional sweetmeats. The second day of the festival is known as the "awning ceremony," during which families gather to cook traditional foods and exchange gifts. Many households also engage in spring cleaning and decorate their homes with colorful decorations and rangolis. Other traditional activities during the Sinhala and Tamil New Year include playing traditional games such as Kana Mutti (breaking a clay pot blindfolded), climbing a greased pole, and tug-of-war. These games are a way for people to come together, have fun, and celebrate the spirit of community. The Sinhala and Tamil New Year is a time for celebrating the richness of Sri Lanka's cultural heritage and for promoting unity and harmony among its diverse communities. The festival is an important time for families and friends to come together, exchange gifts, and celebrate the start of a new year.



May

Vesak


Vesak, also known as Buddha Purnima or Buddha Jayanti, is a major Buddhist festival celebrated in Sri Lanka and other countries around the world. The festival is held on the full moon day in the month of Vesakha, which usually falls in May. The festival commemorates the birth, enlightenment, and death of the Buddha, the founder of Buddhism. It is a time for Buddhists to reflect on the teachings of the Buddha and to strive towards spiritual enlightenment. In Sri Lanka, Vesak is a public holiday and is celebrated with great enthusiasm and devotion. Homes and public buildings are decorated with colorful lanterns and Buddhist flags, and pandals (temporary structures) are set up in towns and cities to depict scenes from the life of the Buddha. Buddhist temples are also decorated with lights and flowers, and devotees gather to offer prayers, light candles and incense, and make offerings to the Buddha. Many people also observe sil (a period of meditation and reflection) and engage in acts of charity and kindness, such as offering food to the poor and needy. A highlight of the Vesak festival is the dansal (free food stall), where people can enjoy free vegetarian meals and refreshments. This is a way for communities to come together, share their blessings, and promote harmony and goodwill. Overall, Vesak is a significant festival in Sri Lanka and is a time for Buddhists to celebrate the life and teachings of the Buddha, and to renew their commitment to the path of spiritual enlightenment. It is also a time for people to come together in a spirit of generosity, kindness, and harmony.

June

Poson


Poson, also known as Poson Poya, is a major Buddhist festival celebrated in Sri Lanka, usually in the month of June. The festival commemorates the introduction of Buddhism to Sri Lanka by the Arahat Mahinda, the son of Emperor Ashoka, in the 3rd century BCE. The festival is celebrated with great enthusiasm and devotion, particularly in the ancient city of Anuradhapura, which was the capital of Sri Lanka during the time of the introduction of Buddhism. Thousands of pilgrims flock to Anuradhapura to visit the sacred sites associated with the Arahat Mahinda and to offer prayers and make offerings at Buddhist temples. During Poson, many households and public buildings are decorated with colorful lanterns and Buddhist flags. Temples are also decorated with flowers and lights, and special ceremonies and processions are held to mark the occasion. One of the most important aspects of the Poson festival is the practice of sil (virtuous conduct), which involves observing certain precepts such as abstaining from meat and alcohol and engaging in acts of kindness and generosity. Many people also observe a period of meditation and reflection during the festival. Poson is a time for Buddhists to reflect on the importance of the Buddha's teachings, and to renew their commitment to the path of spiritual enlightenment. It is also a time for people to come together in a spirit of harmony and goodwill, and to celebrate the rich cultural heritage of Sri Lanka.



July

Eseala Perahara


Esala Perahera is a grand cultural and religious festival celebrated annually in Sri Lanka, usually in July or August. The festival is held to honor the sacred tooth relic of the Buddha, which is enshrined in the Temple of the Tooth in the ancient city of Kandy. The festival is a ten-day event, featuring a procession of caparisoned elephants, dancers, drummers, and other performers, who parade through the streets of Kandy, accompanied by a host of colorful lights and fireworks. The first five days of the festival are dedicated to the preliminary rituals and ceremonies, while the next five days are devoted to the grand procession of the sacred tooth relic. The procession features beautifully decorated elephants, dressed in colorful costumes and adorned with ornate jewelry and decorations, carrying the sacred tooth relic in a golden casket. The procession also includes dancers, drummers, fire-eaters, and other performers, creating a vibrant and spectacular spectacle. The Esala Perahera festival is one of the most significant events in Sri Lanka, attracting large crowds of locals and tourists alike. It is a time for people to come together in a spirit of devotion and celebration, and to pay homage to the sacred tooth relic of the Buddha. Overall, the Esala Perahera festival is a wonderful example of Sri Lanka's rich cultural heritage, and it is a testament to the country's strong Buddhist traditions and customs.

August

Ramadan


Ramadan is an important religious festival celebrated by the Muslim community in Sri Lanka and around the world. It is the ninth month of the Islamic calendar and is observed by fasting from dawn until sunset. During Ramadan, Muslims in Sri Lanka wake up before dawn to eat a pre-dawn meal called suhoor, and then fast throughout the day until sunset, when they break their fast with a meal called iftar. The fast is an act of worship and is intended to help Muslims develop self-discipline, patience, and empathy for those who are less fortunate. In Sri Lanka, Muslims observe Ramadan with great devotion and enthusiasm. Mosques and homes are decorated with lights and colorful decorations, and people come together to share iftar meals and engage in acts of charity and goodwill. Many Muslim communities in Sri Lanka also hold special prayers and religious gatherings during Ramadan, known as taraweeh prayers. These prayers are held after the evening iftar meal and provide an opportunity for Muslims to reflect on the teachings of Islam and to strengthen their faith. Overall, Ramadan is an important festival for Muslims in Sri Lanka and is a time for people to come together in a spirit of devotion, kindness, and compassion. It is a reminder of the importance of self-discipline and the need to care for those who are less fortunate, and it is a time to renew one's commitment to the teachings of Islam.



November

Deepavali


Deepavali, also known as Diwali, is a major festival celebrated by Hindus, Sikhs, and Jains around the world, including in Sri Lanka. The festival usually falls in the month of October or November and is celebrated over a period of five days. Deepavali is a festival of lights, and it symbolizes the victory of good over evil, light over darkness, and knowledge over ignorance. The festival is celebrated by lighting diyas (earthen lamps) and candles, decorating homes and public places with colorful rangolis, and bursting firecrackers. In Sri Lanka, Deepavali is celebrated by the Hindu community with great enthusiasm and devotion. People clean and decorate their homes, wear new clothes, and offer prayers and offerings to the deities. They also prepare and share sweets and other delicacies with their family, friends, and neighbors. One of the most important aspects of Deepavali is the practice of exchanging gifts and greetings with loved ones. It is a time for people to come together in a spirit of joy and celebration and to strengthen their bonds of friendship and love. Overall, Deepavali is a wonderful celebration of the triumph of light over darkness and of the power of goodness over evil. It is a time for people to come together, to share love and kindness, and to celebrate the richness and diversity of Sri Lanka's cultural heritage.

December

Christmas


Christmas is a significant festival for Christians in Sri Lanka, who make up around 7% of the population. It is celebrated on December 25th each year and is a time for Christians to come together and celebrate the birth of Jesus Christ. In Sri Lanka, Christians celebrate Christmas by attending special church services, singing carols, and decorating their homes and public spaces with Christmas trees, lights, and other festive decorations. People also exchange gifts and greetings with their loved ones, and many prepare special meals and treats to share with family and friends. In addition to the religious celebrations, Christmas in Sri Lanka is also a time for cultural festivities and entertainment. The city of Colombo, for example, is known for its colorful and vibrant Christmas markets, where people can enjoy traditional Sri Lankan food and drinks, listen to music, and purchase gifts and souvenirs. Overall, Christmas is an important festival for Christians in Sri Lanka and is a time for people to come together in a spirit of joy and celebration. It is a time to reflect on the meaning of Christmas and to share love, kindness, and goodwill with one another.
